SDLC (Software Development Life Cycle)

The software development lifecycle (SDLC) is a process/method that a developer/team of development uses in order to build high quality softwares.The aim of SDLC is to reduce any project risks by forward planning so that it meets expectations of the customers even after tghe completion of the project. It consists series of steps that divides the process of development into several parts that you can assign or complete in the given time frame. It generally consists of the following steps:

  1. Plan
  2. Design
  3. Implement
  4. Test
  5. Deploy
  6. Maintain
In today's digital era, every technology is getting rapid upgraded, hence it is difficult to maintain and manage the constant changing requirements. This is where SDLC comes into play. It provides a systematic management framework with specific deliverables at every stage of the software development process. As a result, all stakeholders agree on software development goals and requirements upfront and also have a plan to achieve those goals.

Preferred SDLC Methods
Method Information about method
Waterfall Requirements, design, implementation, testing, deployment, and maintenance phases that must all be finished before moving on to the next in a linear and sequential manner.
Agile Its the new age methodology a progressive and iterative strategy that prioritizes consumer feedback, flexibility, and teamwork. Agile divides development into sprints, which are brief iterations.
DevOps Focuses on continuous integration and continuous delivery (CI/CD) by combining development (Dev) and operations (Ops) teams to improve cooperation and automate procedures.
Spiral Model Blends risk-driven and iterative methods. With an emphasis on risk analysis at every stage, development proceeds in recurring cycles, or spirals.
Incremental Model Enables the system to be created and delivered one increment at a time, breaking it up into smaller parts.
RAD (Rapid Application Development) Focuses on developing prototypes fast and making adjustments in response to user input.

Even among all these methodologies, I would prefer Agile. And even in Agile I would choose the following:

  1. Kanban: A visual Agile approach that manages work using a Kanban board. emphasizes continuous delivery rather than set sprints. Best suited for teams that don't require time-boxed iterations and prioritize flexibility.
  2. Scrum: A branch of Agile that emphasizes sprints and well defined roles (e.g., Product Owner, Scrum Master). The work is arranged in brief, time-boxed iterations, usually lasting two to four weeks. Ideal for groups seeking regular feedback cycles and organization.
  3. Extreme Programming (XP): Engineering techniques to enhance software quality and adaptability to evolving needs. Ideal for groups who require superior technical performance and deliverables in hectic settings.
  4. Feature-driven development (FDD): Iteratively developing and delivering features while adhering to a well defined methodology. Ideal for big groups working on intricate projects where the main output is features.
  5. Lean: This strategy, which was influenced by lean manufacturing, emphasizes producing value through waste reduction and process optimization. Suitable for groups seeking low overhead and simple procedures.
